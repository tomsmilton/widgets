CUSTOM AIR FILTRATION SYSTEM
REF: ENG-2025-03-02 | ENG: T. GREEN, M. FARROW

Overview

This log documents our engineering design process for developing a custom air filtration system for office environments. Key focus areas include maximizing air change rates, minimizing noise, optimizing cost-effectiveness, and creating an aesthetically acceptable design.

PERFORMANCE METRICS
> Target airflow:           1000 m³/hr (600 CFM)
> Achieved airflow:         1260 m³/hr (741 CFM)
> Operating noise level:    < 30 dBA
> Air changes per hour:     4 ACH (target), 5.25 ACH (achieved)
> Cost reduction:           > 50% vs. commercial alternatives

Our custom-built solution successfully outperformed commercial alternatives in both performance and cost-effectiveness while meeting our specific environmental and aesthetic requirements. This report details our comprehensive engineering process from initial concept to final prototype testing.

Initial Concept Exploration

Our investigation began with an analysis of existing solutions, particularly Clean Air Kits designs that use PC cooling fans to power air filtration. We identified several opportunities for improvement:

  • Using larger fans (140mm vs 120mm) that could operate at lower RPM for the same airflow
  • Optimizing the fan arrangement to improve performance
  • Creating a more aesthetically pleasing and office-appropriate design
  • Reducing overall cost

Initial CAD concepts were developed based on a box-like structure with multiple PC fans and standard air conditioning filters.

Figure 1: Initial CAD concept with PC fans and AC filters

The Clean Air Kits units use MPR 1900 AC filters, which are readily available at approximately £45 each with a recommended replacement interval of 9 months. These filters provide good filtration area at a reasonable cost.

Technical Analysis

To properly select fans and design the system, we needed to understand the pressure-flow relationship of our filters. Testing revealed that our filters followed this pressure curve:

FILTER PRESSURE EQUATION
P = 142 × Q^{1.39}

Where:
P = pressure in Pascals
Q = flow rate in m³/s
Figure 2: Filter pressure drop curve

For our target flow rate of 1000m³/hr through a single filter, this would create a pressure loss of approximately 23.9Pa. With two filters in parallel (each handling 500m³/hr), the pressure loss would drop to approximately 9.13Pa per filter.

Sound Level Considerations

Fan noise increases non-linearly with the number of fans. For n fans, the total sound pressure level can be calculated as:

NOISE CALCULATION
L_{total} = L_{single} + 10log_{10}(n)

Where:
L_{total} = total noise level (dBA)
L_{single} = single fan noise level (dBA)
n = number of fans

Another important factor is the relationship between fan speed and noise. Testing and literature showed that noise increases with the fifth power of speed:

NOISE-SPEED RELATIONSHIP
Noise = 10 × log_{10}((v/v_{max})^5)

Where:
v = operating speed
v_{max} = maximum speed
Figure 3: Noise-speed relationship from HSE data

This means that running fans at 80% speed instead of 100% reduces noise by approximately 5dB—a substantial improvement in perceived loudness.

Material Analysis

Filter Covering Pressure Loss

Our investigation into speaker cloth revealed a significant concern: additional pressure loss. Based on analysis, typical speaker cloth could create 15-20Pa of pressure loss, which was even higher than the pressure loss from our filters (9.13Pa)!

Optimization Analysis

We developed a computational model to optimize for performance per unit cost while maintaining noise constraints below 30dBA. Results were visualized for configurations with and without fabric covering:

Figure 15: Optimization results with fabric covering
Figure 16: Optimization results without fabric covering
OPTIMAL CONFIGURATIONS
WITH FABRIC COVERING:
- 8× Arctic F14 fans at 90% speed: 339 CFM
- 7× Arctic F14 fans at 90% speed: 313 CFM
- 6× Arctic F14 fans at 90% speed: 284 CFM

WITHOUT FABRIC COVERING:
- 9× Arctic F14 fans at 90% speed: 440 CFM
- 8× Arctic F14 fans at 90% speed: 406 CFM

Our most significant finding was that multiple smaller units would outperform a single large unit due to the nonlinear relationship between pressure and airflow. This led us to pivot from a single large "radiator" design to two smaller cabinet-style units, each with two filters and 8 fans per unit.

Conclusions and Future Improvements

Our engineering process led to a highly optimized air filtration system that delivers:

KEY ACHIEVEMENTS
✓ Airflow of 741 CFM (1260m³/hr) without fabric covering
✓ Noise levels under 30dBA at operating speed
✓ Total cost ~£600 for two units (< 50% of commercial alternatives)
✓ Easily serviceable design with simple filter replacement
✓ Modern, minimalist aesthetic appropriate for office environments

The most significant findings from this project were:

  1. Multiple smaller units are more efficient than one large unit due to the nonlinear relationship between pressure and flow
  2. PC cooling fans provide an excellent balance of performance, noise, and cost for this application
  3. Filter covering materials have a dramatically larger impact on performance than initially expected
  4. A systematic, data-driven approach to fan selection and system design produces superior results compared to intuition-based design

Future work should address the Arctic F14 PWM PST fans end-of-life status, which highlights a risk with consumer/gaming-grade components. Future designs should consider industrial components with longer availability guarantees.
