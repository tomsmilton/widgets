Clean Air Kits Review

We began by analyzing the commercially available Clean Air Kits. These units leverage PC fans, which have benefited from extensive R&D to optimize for quiet operation, making them perfect for powering an air filtration device. However:

  • The commercial kits are expensive, approximately £1,300 to outfit our office (including postage), and their aesthetic design is suboptimal
  • Raw cost is approximately £1,000
  • Replacement filters cost around £300

Alternative Approach

I've developed a basic CAD concept that adapts the Clean Air Kits design with several improvements:

  • Using the same filter type but with one additional fan and larger fans overall (140mm vs 120mm)
  • Operating the larger fans at slightly lower RPM to maintain quiet operation while achieving higher flow rates
Initial CAD concept design

The proposed construction consists of:

  • Laser-cut plywood and 3D printed parts that slot together
  • Electronics (power supply, adapters, PWM fan splitters, fans) costing approximately £70 per unit
  • Arctic P14 fans, which are well-regarded for quiet operation and good performance against high static pressures - we can acquire 5 units for £33 on Amazon

While this initial design is functional rather than aesthetic, the final model would have a cleaner appearance.

Filter Selection

I plan to use the same filters as Clean Air Kits:

  • Filtrete Ultimate Allergen Reduction Filters

Alternative filter options with similar specifications are available at a lower cost:

  • Filtrete MERV 13 Filters - These filter slightly smaller particles but are very similar and cost approximately £30 less per unit

I'm considering adding a 60 mesh (0.25mm aperture) metal filter layer on the outside of the primary filters to improve aesthetics and extend filter life by keeping coarser particles out. Ideally, these outer mesh layers would be removable:

  • Inoxia 60 Mesh Stainless Steel

Several filter aspect ratios are available, depending on the selected filter size versus the number of fans. Common filter sizes in inches include:

  • 14×20
  • 16×20
  • 16×25
  • 18×24
  • 20×20
  • 20×25
  • 20×30
  • 24×30

To minimize noise, I'll mount the fans on the inner wall of the panel using rubber washers and add sound-dampening foam on the two passive walls.

Filter Analysis

The Clean Air Kits unit uses a MPR 1900 AC filter. These filters cost approximately £45 each and require replacement every 9 months. These AC filters are a good option due to their decent availability and reasonable cost for the filtration area achieved.

Future improvements could include adding an activated carbon layer for odor control. The removable metal mesh will extend the life of the primary filter by preventing dust clogging while significantly improving aesthetics.

We're targeting 4 air changes per hour (ACH) for our office space. With estimated dimensions of 8×12×2.5m = 240m³, we require approximately 1000m³/hr or 600CFM airflow.

Filter Pressure-Flow Relationship

FILTER PRESSURE-FLOW EQUATION
P = 142 × Q1.39

where:
P = pressure in pascals
Q = flow rate in m³/s

This power-law relationship means that as we increase flow rate, the pressure drop increases non-linearly, which has significant implications for fan selection.

Filter pressure drop curve

Filter Selection Analysis

I've spent considerable time investigating different filter types. The Filtrete panels appear to be the optimal balance between undersized home solutions and large, bulky HVAC bag filters.

Standard home HEPA filters claim 1 year of filtration each at around 200m³/hr. In contrast, Filtrete MPR 2200 filters recommend replacement every 3 months with a maximum rating of 3000m³/hr (though they're not true HEPA). The quarter-life at 10x airflow seems reasonable, and these filters still capture PM2.5 particles effectively. We would need to add carbon filtration separately if desired.

There are numerous schemes for measuring filter capture efficiency, which complicates comparisons.

F7, F8 & F9 Fine Bag Filters

These are high-grade synthetic bag air filters for ventilation systems requiring a high standard of cleanliness, including offices, schools, hospitals, and shopping centers.

Where greater dust holding capacity is required than a panel filter can offer, these High Efficiency Bag Filters' large pockets accommodate this while providing lower resistance.

The High Performance Bag Air Filter design ensures long life between maintenance intervals.

For capturing PM2.5 particles, we'd want a grade F7, F8, or F9 filter. However, many HVAC-oriented filters are designed for pressure drops (100-250Pa) that exceed what PC fans can handle, which is why I'm recommending we stick with Filtrete.

HEPA Filtration Analysis

True HEPA filtration is typically specified for cleanrooms and is likely overkill for our application.

Filters Direct supplies a 24×24×6" filter rated for 1275m³/hr at a pressure loss of 250Pa.

Energy calculation:

  • 1275m³ × 250Pa = 319kJ
  • 319kJ / 3600 seconds = 88.5W air power
  • Assuming 50% blower efficiency, we're looking at 170W continuous draw
  • Over a 3-month filter life, that's 367kWh, approximately £100 in electricity costs

The filter itself costs £134.75 ex. VAT.

Alternatively, this filter would require:

  • 24×36×5" dimensions
  • £258 per filter
  • Airflow rating of 905m³/hr @ 55Pa
  • 49.775kJ/hr = 14W air power, 28W electrical input
  • 60.5kWh; £16.35 electricity cost over a 3-month lifespan

HEPA filters generally come in two categories - domestic/home and cleanroom grade. If we require true HEPA filtration, we should consider purchasing commercial off-the-shelf units for each room, though these tend to become quite loud at high flow rates.

Sound Level Analysis

SOUND LEVEL CALCULATION
Ltotal = Lsingle + 10 log10(n)

where:
Ltotal = total noise level (dBA)
Lsingle = single fan noise level (dBA)
n = number of fans

In our current unit design with 7 Arctic P14 PWM fans rated at 0.3 Sone each (22.50dBA), the total noise output should be 30.95dBA. This calculation helps us determine acceptable combinations of fans, potentially allowing designs with fewer but louder fans if they stay below this threshold.

Noise-Speed Relationship

To determine whether multiple inexpensive fans running at reduced speeds would outperform premium silent fans at full speed, I investigated the relationship between fan speed and noise. While manufacturers rarely publish SPL/speed charts, Wikipedia indicates that noise increases with speed to the fifth power, meaning halving the speed reduces SPL by approximately 15dB. This relationship is derived from HSE guidance.

Fan noise as a function of speed (Source: HSE)
NOISE-SPEED RELATIONSHIP
Noise = 10 × log10((v/vmax)5)

Target Sound Level

We know the 7 Arctic P14 fans sound acceptable at 9V (vs. 12V nominal), and flow rate is approximately linear with voltage. Assuming RPM is also linearly proportional to voltage:

TARGET NOISE CALCULATION
Ndesired = 30.95 + 10log10((9/12)5) ≈ 24.7dBA

This gives us our target noise level for design iterations.

data.close()

for row in fan_data:
    #Create a new Fan instance for each row in the data file
    fan = Fan(row[0], float(row[1]), float(row[2]), float(row[3]), float(row[4]), float(row[5]), float(row[6]),[])
    
    #Find the minimum number of fans needed to meet the desired flow
    min_qty = round(desired_flow / fan.get_flow(desired_pressure))

    #Reject any fans that are not powerful enough to meet the desired flow
    if min_qty < 0:
        print(f"{fan.name}" + " does not generate enough static pressure to meet the desired flow.")
        continue
    
    #Find operating points for various quantities of each fan
    for numFans in range(min_qty, min_qty*2):
        fan.operating_points.append(OperatingPoint(numFans, (min_qty/numFans), fan.noise))

    fans.append(fan)

    for fan in fans:
        if fan.operating_points:
            print(f"{fan.name} minimum quantity: {fan.operating_points[0].quantity}")

fig, ax = plt.subplots()

# Plot operating point noise against cost for all fans
for fan in fans:
    quantities = [op.quantity for op in fan.operating_points]
    costs = [op.quantity * fan.cost for op in fan.operating_points]
    noises = [op.noiseTotal for op in fan.operating_points]

Fan class implementation:

class Fan:
    def __init__(self, name, diameter, max_flow, static_pressure, power, noise, cost, operating_points):
        self.name = name
        self.diameter = diameter
        self.max_flow = max_flow
        self.static_pressure = static_pressure
        self.power = power
        self.noise = noise
        self.cost = cost
        self.m = -max_flow / static_pressure
        self.c = max_flow
        self.operating_points = operating_points

    def get_flow(self, pressure):
        return self.c + (self.m * pressure)

Operating point calculations:

import math

class OperatingPoint:
    def __init__(self, quantity, speed, ratedNoise):
        self.quantity = quantity
        self.speed = speed
        self.ratedNoise = ratedNoise
        self.noiseEach = getDeratedNoise(self)
        self.noiseTotal = getTotalNoise(self)

def getDeratedNoise(self):
    return self.ratedNoise + (10 * math.log10(self.speed**5))

def getTotalNoise(self):
    return self.noiseEach + (10 * math.log10(self.quantity))

Cost Optimization Analysis

To address Matt's feedback on determining the optimal design approach, I've developed code to plot flow/£ for all permutations with noise <30dBA. Previous analysis optimized for a fixed flow rate.

I now need to calculate the actual flow rate for various filter and fan combinations by solving:

FLOW RATE EQUATION
mF^k + F = c - mj

where:
m = fan pressure coefficient
F = flow rate
c = no-load fan flow
j = filter pressure

I implemented an iterative approach with increasing CFM:

#@return: Flow in CFM, to the nearest 1 CFM
def get_flow(fan:Fan, num_fans:int, speed:float, filter:Filter, num_filters:int):
    max_flow = fan.max_flow*num_fans
    pstat = fan.static_pressure

    #Start at low flow, and increase until pressures roughly match
    flow: float  = 1 #CFM

    #Multiply free-flow and derating (m) coefficients by number of fans
    cAdj: float = fan.c*num_fans*(speed/100.0)
    mAdj: float = fan.m*num_fans*(speed/100.0)

    #Increase flow rate until pressures match
    while(flow < 1e4): #If we hit 1e4, something is wrong!

        #Calculate fan pressure
        pFan: float = (flow-cAdj) / mAdj

        #Calculate filter pressure
        pFilt = 2 * ((flow*CFMtoM3s/num_filters)**filter.exponent) * filter.coeff

        #print(f"Flow: {flow}CFM, Fan Pressure: {pFan}Pa, Filter Pressure: {pFilt}Pa")

        if(pFilt > pFan):
            #Return the *previous* flow, ie. when the ratio was still positive
            return flow-1
        
        #Increase flow
        flow = flow + 1

OPEN AREA CALCULATION

This relatively low open area (80.7%) for such coarse mesh is surprising. It raises questions about whether a finer mesh with equal open area would provide similar visual transparency.

Camira Fabric Resistance Analysis

Since the Camira Cara fabric reduced flow rate by a factor of 3, I calculated its flow resistance:

  • Estimated actual flow rate: ~0.05m³/s (370CFM) - approximately half of the meter reading based on previous calibration
  • This equates to 0.025m³/s per filter
  • Our calculations predict 8 fans at full speed should deliver 391CFM with the fabric's doubled pressure loss
  • For Arctic F14 PWM fans, maximum flow is rated at 73CFM with maximum pressure of 15.7Pa
  • The per-fan flow rate is approximately 46CFM if divided equally
  • From the P/Q curve, this suggests a pressure drop of 0.8mmH₂O (7.85Pa)
  • Expected pressure across the filters alone is 0.84Pa
  • This indicates the fabric introduces approximately 7Pa of pressure loss - nearly 10 times more than the filters themselves
